    • Literature

    Horror literature's evolution has been shaped by whatever a society most fears at the given time. Before the genre had a name, writers were exploring witches, ghosts, monsters and the uncanny alike, with gothic fiction opening the way to modern horror storytelling in the 18th century. The template for horror literature was established by The Castle of Otranto by Horace Walpole: a dark and atmospheric story build around ancient secrets, the supernatural and dread.

    Horror literature became more psychologically complex in the 19th century, with Mary Shelley's Frankeinstein changing the genre by basing its terror partly in science. Meanwhile, Edgar Allan Poe's horror was more inward, with fear based on obsession, decay and the unstable mind. The supernatural genre was also expanded, with writers such as Sheridan Le Fany, Robert Louis Stevenson, Oscar Wilde and Bram Stoker continuing to explore the haunted house, vampires and the corrupted self. By the late 19th and early 20th centuries, horror literature had become a way to explore social anxieties as much as supernatural ones. Bram Stoker's Dracula and Henry James's The Turn of The Screw showed how horror could be entertaining as well as symbolic, reflecting fears about class, uncertainty and sexuality. At the same time, authors like H.P. Lovecraft pushed cosmic horror: the idea that the universe itself was unknowable and hostile.

    By the late 20th century, horror literature broadened into many forms: supernatural and occult, dark fantasy, Sci-Fi horror, and the increasingly popular psychological horror that rooted itself in ordinary life. Other modern threats, such as serial killers, body horror and collapse of society and every day life also became a popular subject.

    What makes horror literature so enduring is adaptability: surviving by changing its shape, influenced by its era's societal and personal fears and turning them into personal, unsettling stories. It continues to remind readers that the most frightening subjects are the ones that reveal truth about our human nature.
